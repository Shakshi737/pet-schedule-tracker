Ticks & Fleas Information

Learn about ticks and fleas, their impact on your pet's health, and effective prevention methods.

Understanding Ticks & Fleas

Ticks and fleas are external parasites that can cause significant discomfort and health problems for your dog. These tiny pests can transmit diseases, cause skin irritation, and lead to infections if not properly managed.

About Ticks

Ticks are arachnids that feed on the blood of animals and humans. Unlike fleas, ticks attach firmly to the skin and can feed for several days. They're typically found in wooded, brushy areas with tall grass and leaf litter.

Common Types of Ticks:

  • Brown Dog Tick: Can complete its entire lifecycle indoors and is found worldwide.
  • American Dog Tick: Common in areas with little or no tree cover, such as grassy fields and hiking trails.
  • Deer Tick (Black-legged tick): Found in wooded areas and can transmit Lyme disease.
  • Lone Star Tick: Aggressive tick that can transmit ehrlichiosis.

Diseases Transmitted by Ticks:

  • Lyme Disease
  • Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever
  • Ehrlichiosis
  • Anaplasmosis
  • Babesiosis
  • Tick Paralysis

About Fleas

Fleas are small, wingless insects that feed on blood. They're excellent jumpers and can quickly infest your home and your pet. A single female flea can lay up to 50 eggs per day, leading to rapid population growth.

Flea Life Cycle:

  • Eggs: Laid on the host, they fall off into the environment (bedding, carpet, etc.)
  • Larvae: Hatch from eggs and feed on organic debris and flea dirt
  • Pupae: Develop into cocoons that are resistant to insecticides
  • Adults: Emerge from cocoons and jump onto hosts to feed and reproduce

Problems Caused by Fleas:

  • Flea Allergy Dermatitis (FAD)
  • Anemia (in severe infestations)
  • Tapeworms (if dogs ingest infected fleas while grooming)
  • Bartonellosis (Cat Scratch Disease)

Signs & Symptoms

Signs of Tick Infestation

  • Visible ticks on skin (often found around ears, neck, head, and between toes)
  • Small bumps on the skin
  • Tick bite scabs
  • Head shaking (if ticks are in the ears)
  • Fever or lethargy (if infected with tick-borne disease)
  • Joint pain or swelling
  • Loss of appetite
  • Weakness or sudden lameness

Signs of Flea Infestation

  • Excessive scratching, biting, or licking
  • Hair loss, especially along the back and tail base
  • Red, irritated skin or rash
  • Small, dark specks in fur or bedding ("flea dirt")
  • Visible fleas in fur, especially in the groin, armpits, and ears
  • Pale gums (in severe cases due to anemia)
  • Restlessness
  • Behavioral changes due to discomfort

Prevention Methods

Prevention is the best approach to protecting your dog from ticks and fleas. Here are effective methods to keep these parasites away:

Oral Medications

Monthly chewable tablets that kill fleas and ticks when they bite your dog. These are convenient and not affected by bathing or swimming.

Topical Treatments

Applied to the skin, these medications spread over your dog's body and kill or repel parasites. Effective for 30 days but may wash off with frequent bathing.

Flea & Tick Collars

Impregnated with chemicals that repel parasites. Some can provide protection for up to 8 months. Good for dogs that swim frequently.

Sprays & Powders

Can provide temporary protection against fleas and ticks. Great for immediate relief but typically need frequent reapplication.

Home Environment Treatment

Regular vacuuming, washing of pet bedding, yard maintenance, and occasionally using household or yard treatments to eliminate eggs and larvae.

Regular Checks

Examining your dog's coat and skin regularly, especially after walks in wooded or grassy areas, to catch and remove parasites early.

Prevention Schedule

For most areas, flea and tick prevention is recommended year-round, but is especially crucial during warm months (spring through fall). Set calendar reminders to administer preventative treatments on time.

Treatment & Removal

How to Remove a Tick

  1. Use fine-tipped tweezers or a tick removal tool.
  2. Grasp the tick as close to the skin's surface as possible.
  3. Pull upward with steady, even pressure. Don't twist or jerk.
  4. After removal, clean the bite area with alcohol or soap and water.
  5. Dispose of the live tick by placing it in alcohol, sealing it in a bag, or flushing it down the toilet.

Important: Never crush a tick with your fingers, and avoid folk remedies like nail polish or petroleum jelly.

Treating Flea Infestations

Step 1: Treat Your Pet

  • Apply a vet-recommended flea treatment product to your dog.
  • Give your pet a bath with a flea shampoo if recommended by your vet.
  • Comb your dog with a fine-toothed flea comb, dipping the comb in a bowl of soapy water between passes.

Step 2: Treat Your Home

  • Vacuum thoroughly and frequently, especially in areas where your pet spends time.
  • Wash all bedding, toys, and other washable items in hot water.
  • Consider using a household flea spray or fogger for severe infestations.
  • Treat your yard if necessary with appropriate outdoor products.

Step 3: Prevention

  • Continue regular preventative treatments.
  • Monitor your pet for signs of reinfestation.
  • Keep your home clean and vacuumed regularly.

Product Recommendations

Here are some veterinarian-recommended products for flea and tick prevention:

Oral Preventatives

Monthly chewable tablets that kill fleas and ticks.

Examples: Bravecto, NexGard, Simparica

Topical Treatments

Applied to skin for month-long protection.

Examples: Frontline Plus, Advantage II, K9 Advantix II

Flea & Tick Collars

Long-lasting protection for up to 8 months.

Examples: Seresto, Preventic

Shampoos & Sprays

For immediate treatment of existing infestations.

Examples: Adams Plus, Vet's Best

Note: Always consult with your veterinarian before starting any parasite prevention program. The effectiveness of products may vary based on your location and your pet's specific needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I treat my dog for fleas and ticks?

Most oral and topical preventatives need to be administered monthly. Some newer products can last up to 3 months, and certain collars can provide protection for up to 8 months. Follow the manufacturer's instructions and your vet's recommendations.

Is it necessary to treat for fleas and ticks in winter?

In many regions, it's recommended to treat year-round. While ticks may be less active in cold temperatures, they can become active during brief warm spells. Fleas can survive indoors during any season. Your veterinarian can provide guidance based on your specific location.

Can my dog get fleas or ticks if they rarely go outside?

Yes, parasites can be brought into your home on clothing or by other pets. Fleas can jump from wildlife or neighboring yards into your home environment, and once inside, they can multiply rapidly.

Are natural remedies effective against fleas and ticks?

Most natural remedies have limited scientific evidence supporting their effectiveness. While some (like certain essential oils) may offer some repellent effects, they typically don't provide the same level of protection as veterinary-approved products. Additionally, some natural substances can be toxic to dogs, so always consult your vet before using them.

What should I do if my dog has a reaction to a flea or tick product?

If you notice symptoms like excessive scratching, redness, hair loss, vomiting, or lethargy after applying a product, contact your veterinarian immediately. Bathe your dog with mild soap if it's a topical product (unless instructed otherwise by your vet). Always keep the product packaging for reference.
